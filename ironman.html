The Ironman

Work in progress... Merging computer science and robotics with the new energy source, the Ironman suit is the advancement we need to help both civilians and astronauts to resist to harsh environments and lift heavy weights both at work or during rescue missions (e.g. to save people trapped after an earthquake). Plus, it's darn cool!
The biggest challenges to build it are:
- How should it execute any movement? What is the input here? What is the output?
- Can we multiply the strength of the user? If so, what approach works best?
- How do we make it fly?
- Lastly, is it possible to build Jarvis?

Let's move

In order to realize robotic movements we have available what are called as "actuators". We can use those to move the suit and multiply the strength of the user. Some examples have already been realized: see name or name experiments (MIT and guy that lifts heavy weights). Yes, the movements may not be very fluid, yet they do their job, so it's just a matter of tuning the settings to make the exoskeleton move as wanted.
What is important here is to decide how to read the input, the will of the user to move in one direction or the other. It can be done using pressure sensors. They are cheap enough and easy to find or realize. This solution, though, would make it impossible to, for example, lay an arm on the table during a break, because the sensors around the suit would feel the arm trying to move downwards (hello gravity!), so the actuators would react by moving the arm in that direction.
>>Video Indiano exosuit
Muscle sensors, on the other hand, would leave the arm where it stands as long add the muscle is relaxed. Those sensors are more reliable, but Aust the same time need to be placed in contact with the arm all the time. To do so we could attach them with sticky patches or use non-glued reusable patches while keeping them in place with an elastic band.
After this, we have to choose what kind of actuator we need to use to realize the right output. There are affordable actuators that use electricity to function, but they move slowly. Another kind of actuators work with compressed air and create a considerable force, but need considerable energy, together with an air compressor, which is usually pretty big and not easy to transport. Plus, it needs tubes to carry the air around and this introduces a vulnerability in the system. It doesn't seem practical.
In my opinion, a good actuator should work on electricity only, limiting the potential vulnerability of the system and, thus, guaranteeing the highest reliability. This brings out a problem: how do wet make powerful electric actuators?
